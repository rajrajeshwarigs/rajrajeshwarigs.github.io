Posture Coach

Final Year Project, August 2020 - May 2021

Posture Coach is a group final-year capstone project for the Bachelor's of Engineering in Computer Science at Dayananda Sagar College of Engineering

TL;DR

Posture Coach is a cross platform mobile application that aims to help prevent injuries and improve the quality of people’s workouts with just a smartphone, that uses the system’s inbuilt camera to detect the user’s exercise pose and provide personalized, detailed recommendations on how the user can improve their form in real time.

The Inspiration

Fitness is a vital component of our health and well-being. Due to the circumstances caused by the covid-19 pandemic, most people do not have access to a gym or fitness equipment; hence many people have resorted to exercising by themselves at home. People have become dependent on technology to receive appropriate fitness guidance and training.

Since most gyms have been closed, people have now resorted to working out at their homes instead. Exercises such as squats, deadlifts, and shoulder presses are beneficial to health and fitness, but they can also be very dangerous if performed incorrectly. Most people need guidance to maintain the correct posture while exercising. Technology can be used to monitor and provide this guidance by making use of Pose Estimation.

Design and Implementation

Pose Estimation

The PoseNet algorithm is a pre-trained model for pose estimation. It is available on TensorFlow lite and TensorFlow.JS libraries, which makes it easier to be implemented on mobile device applications. It is designed to run on mobile devices and browsers. It takes a processed camera image as the input and then generates 17 key points of body parts along with their confidence score between 0.0 and 1.0.

Keypoint Normalization

We must create our own parser to use the raw keypoint data after executing pose estimation. We also need to generalise our software to cater for individuals with various body length measurements, camera distance, and other related aspects. We normalise the pose based on the torso's length in pixels to accommodate for these variances. The average distance between the neck keypoint and the right and left hip keypoints is used to compute torso length. Distances are thus expressed as ratios of torso length

Perspective Detection

We must resolve the ambiguity in the camera viewpoint for some workouts. The bicep curl exercise, for example, must be done from the side of the body and can be done with either the left or right arm. We need to figure out which arm is doing the work. By analysing which key points are most apparent (left or right side keypoints) throughout all frames of the activity, we can determine which arm is conducting the exercise in the video. We do this by comparing keypoint confidence scores for each side.

Evaluation

We perform exercise pose evaluation using a geometric approach. We first determine the metrics based on which we must evaluate the user’s posture for each exercise. We compute body vectors from key points of interest and use personal training guidelines to design geometric heuristics and evaluate the body vectors.

Feedback

Our application provides real-time audio and text feedback to the user as they are performing the exercise. Using the results of the evaluation, we provide positive reinforcement messages such as "Good work", "Well done" for a correctly performed exercise and suggestions on how to improve posture such as "you should lift your arm higher", or "squat lower" for an incorrectly performed exercise.

Exercises

Currently, we have implemented the evaluation of 4 exercises - Bicep Curl, Shoulder Press, Front Raise, and Squats, as shown above.
